Low‑Voltage Electroflotation for Algal Clarification

We explore whether low‑voltage electroflotation can clarify algae‑rich, mosquito‑prone water using microbubble‑driven flotation instead of chemicals. Experiments with Chlorella vulgaris in ½‑gallon jars showed that 7.2 V can reduce turbidity to near‑water absorbance within 24 hours.

Why Electroflotation?

Open stormwater drains often accumulate stagnant, nutrient‑rich water that supports algal growth and creates habitats for mosquito larvae. These conditions increase the risk of vector‑borne disease transmission. Chemical larvicides require constant purchase and reapplication, and may be undesirable in low‑maintenance systems.

Mosquito‑Prone Water

Stagnant, nutrient‑rich water drives algal blooms and provides ideal breeding habitats for mosquito larvae.

Limitations of Chemicals

Larvicides and chemical treatments demand repeated application and ongoing cost, and can be unsuitable for intermittent or remote systems.

Electroflotation Opportunity

Microbubble‑driven flotation offers a non‑chemical, energy‑efficient way to lift and remove suspended algal biomass.

System Architecture

Our prototype uses vertically oriented graphite electrodes in ½‑gallon jars, powered by a low‑voltage DC supply and controlled by a MOSFET‑based timing circuit. Electrolysis generates hydrogen and oxygen microbubbles that attach to algal cells, lifting them and clarifying the water.

1. Reactor Module

Two graphite electrodes (13 mm × 105 mm) spaced 0.75 cm apart in ½‑gallon jars containing cultured Chlorella vulgaris.

  • Electrodes ~1 cm above jar bottom
  • Algae grown with AeroGarden nutrient solution
  • Indoor classroom lighting (~300–500 lux)

2. Power & Timing

Bench power supply at 0, 5, 7.2, and 10 V, controlled by an Arduino‑driven MOSFET circuit that activates electrolysis for 5 minutes every 2 hours.

  • Low‑power DC (< 10 V) for safety
  • 12 activation cycles over 24 hours
  • Current draw from ~5.4 mA to 12.5 mA

3. Sensing & Data

Turbidity measured at surface and mid‑depth using a Vernier Colorimeter at 635 nm, with pH tracked qualitatively to monitor electrolysis intensity.

  • Initial turbidity: ~2–4 NTU
  • Sampling at 0, 1, and 24 hours
  • Microscope used to assess aggregation and floc formation

Key Results

A one‑way ANOVA showed a significant effect of voltage on turbidity reduction (F(3,8) = 6.15, p < 0.05), with 7.2 V consistently producing the strongest clarification while avoiding foam and pigment damage seen at 10 V.

7.2 V ~91–100% turbidity reduction; final turbidity < 0.1 NTU at both layers; minimal foaming.
10 V ~89–99% reduction but excessive foaming and pigment damage (browning of biomass).
5 V ~13–65% reduction; sub‑threshold bubble generation and remixing of settled cells.
0 V Natural settling only; jars remained relatively turbid over 24 hours.

Voltage & Current Thresholds

Voltage acted as a convenient proxy for current density with fixed geometry and conductivity, but microbubble generation fundamentally depends on current. Follow‑up trials mapped activation behavior versus current, revealing an optimal zone around 0.177–0.18 A.

Voltage Threshold

7.2 V marks the threshold where microbubble generation becomes strong enough for effective flotation without excessive foaming or pigment damage.

Current Activation Zone

Around 0.177–0.18 A, surface reduction reached ~92–99.5% and mid‑depth reduction ~92–97%, with modest pH shifts (~±0.5 units).

Safe Operating Window

Higher currents (e.g., 0.22–0.262 A) caused pigment destruction and electrode loss, while lower currents (0.16 A) failed to sustain stable microbubble generation.

Future Work

Next steps include current‑controlled, closed‑loop prototypes for stormwater drains, mapping behavior across multiple algal species and mixed organic loads, and eventually integrating mosquito larvae and sludge to emulate real‑world conditions.

Closed‑Loop Control

Use current feedback to automatically adjust voltage and maintain target bubble generation across variable conductivity.

Species & Habitat Expansion

Extend trials beyond Chlorella vulgaris to filamentous algae, mixed communities, and realistic stormwater compositions.

Deployable Modules

Design free‑floating electroflotation units for low‑maintenance installation in open drains and retention basins.
